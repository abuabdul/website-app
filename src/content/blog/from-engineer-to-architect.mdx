## The title change that wasn't

When I started getting involved in architectural decisions, I expected the shift to feel like levelling up — same job, just harder problems. I was wrong.

The change from "engineer who codes" to "architect who shapes systems" is less like levelling up and more like changing games. The skills that made me a great engineer — deep technical knowledge, an eye for clean code, the ability to debug anything — became necessary but no longer sufficient.

## What actually changes

### The time horizon extends

As an engineer, my mental model was mostly local: this function, this service, this sprint. As an architect, I started thinking in quarters and years. What will this decision look like when the team triples? When the codebase is five years old? When requirements we can't anticipate arrive?

This sounds obvious written down, but the shift in practice is disorienting. You start saying "it depends" more — and meaning it.

### The output changes

Engineers ship code. Architects ship *context*.

The most valuable things I produce now aren't pull requests — they're architecture decision records (ADRs), diagrams that make a complex system legible at a glance, conversations that unblock a team by surfacing the real constraint, and documents that let a team make good decisions without me in the room.

This was hard to adjust to. There's no green build notification when you write a good ADR. The feedback loops are long and indirect.

### You become a translator

Half the job is translating between worlds: between what the business needs and what engineering can build, between what an individual engineer wants to do and what the system actually needs, between what's technically correct and what's pragmatically achievable given constraints.

Neither side particularly understands the other's language, and you end up spending a lot of time in that gap.

## What doesn't change

The fundamentals matter more, not less. A broad understanding of distributed systems, data modelling, and performance trade-offs becomes more critical as you move up — because your decisions now affect things at scale.

And the best architects I've met are still deeply technical. They stay hands-on deliberately, not because they're reluctant to let go, but because they know that credibility in the room requires genuine understanding of the work.

## The advice I'd give myself

Start thinking about the "why" behind every architectural choice you encounter, not just the "what". When you see a microservices setup, don't just understand it — ask what problem it was solving and what it costs. When you see a monolith, ask the same.

Build a library of trade-offs, not a library of solutions. The solutions change. The trade-offs are durable.

And write things down. Externalising your thinking is a skill that takes practice, and it's the difference between being the person with all the knowledge and being the person who scales.
